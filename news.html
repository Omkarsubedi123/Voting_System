Historic Voter Turnout in Greenwood High's Student Council Elections

Greenwood High celebrated a record-breaking student turnout during its annual student council elections held this Friday. More than 85% of the student body participated in the vote — a historic milestone in the school’s democratic history. Principal Laura James credited the increase to this year’s digital voting system and the enthusiastic campaigning of all candidates. “The energy this year was electric,” she said. The senior class presidency was won by Jasmine Lee, who emphasized mental health and academic flexibility in her campaign. “I want students to feel heard,” Lee said after her win. Other notable victories included Daniel Cruz as vice president and Priya Narang as treasurer. Faculty advisor Mr. Thompson praised the professionalism of all candidates and encouraged continued involvement in student government. With such strong engagement, the council is hopeful for a productive and inclusive year ahead.

Nomination Process Begins for District Offices

In a first for Westbridge High, students from the computer science club launched a custom-built voting app for the school’s elections this year. The app, named “VoteMate,” allowed students to cast their votes using school-issued tablets or their personal phones. The app featured candidate bios, campaign videos, and a secure login system tied to student IDs. “We wanted to modernize the process and make it accessible to all,” said Liam Park, one of the app’s developers. Teachers praised the initiative for increasing participation and saving paper. Student council advisor Mrs. Ellington said the app boosted voter turnout by nearly 20%. “It made the whole process smoother,” she added. Voters appreciated the simplicity and ability to learn about candidates quickly. The tech team now plans to open-source the app for use in other schools. The election saw newcomer Ava Gonzalez win the presidency, campaigning heavily on digital inclusion and student feedback systems. Her first initiative: expanding access to school Wi-Fi and increasing student tech workshops.

Twin Sisters Go Head-to-Head for Student President at Ridgeview Secondary

For the first time in Ridgeview Secondary’s history, twin sisters Emma and Emily Walters ran against each other for the position of student body president. The two seniors, known for their leadership in school clubs and sports, drew large crowds to their debates and campaign rallies. Emma focused her campaign on academic reform and mental health resources, while Emily emphasized extracurricular funding and event planning. “We may be twins, but we’ve got very different visions,” joked Emily during their final debate. Emily was quick to congratulate her sister, saying, “I’m proud of her — and I’ll still be supporting the student council in every way I can.” Their parents, who attended the results announcement, said they couldn’t be prouder. Student Journalist Uncovers Campaign Funding Discrepancies at Lincoln Prep

A student reporter at Lincoln Preparatory School has sparked debate after publishing an article revealing discrepancies in campaign funding among student council candidates. Writing for The Lion’s Roar, junior Jamie Chen detailed how some candidates received large donations from student clubs and parents, while others relied on personal savings. The exposé raised questions about fairness and transparency. “We need clear rules on what’s allowed,” Chen wrote. The article prompted immediate responses from the administration and current council members, who pledged to review campaign finance guidelines. Some candidates defended their funding sources, while others welcomed the scrutiny. “This is about equal opportunity,” said candidate Tanya Patel, who had a limited campaign budget. Principal Harding said the school will likely introduce donation caps and mandatory disclosures next year. Chen’s work has been praised as a model of student journalism, and her article has been shared in neighboring schools. Meanwhile, the election continues, with debates now including questions about ethics, equity, and campaign spending.

Seniors Protest Rule Change Barring Them from Running in Mid-Year Elections

At Bellmont High, a group of seniors staged a peaceful protest after new rules prohibited them from running in the mid-year student council elections. The updated policy, announced just two weeks before the campaign period, restricts candidates to students who will remain enrolled the following academic year — effectively disqualifying most seniors. “It’s unfair and sudden,” said senior Michael Reyes, who had planned to run for president. Over 60 students signed a petition demanding a reversal of the policy. School officials say the change was intended to ensure council continuity. “We want student leaders who can see projects through,” said Vice Principal Denise Lang. But critics argue that the decision limits leadership opportunities and wasn’t communicated clearly. In response to the protest, a town hall meeting was scheduled for next week. Students hope their voices will be heard. “We’re not asking for favoritism,” said Reyes. “Just fairness.” Meanwhile, the election proceeds with eligible juniors and sophomores preparing their campaigns.
